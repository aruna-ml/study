The Supply Chain Rosetta Stone

Cloud resource provisioning is supply chain management with different nouns. This section teaches you to translate fluently between the two worlds вҖ” and to use that translation as your competitive advantage in the interview.

Why This Is Your Edge, Not Your Gap

When most candidates walk into a distributed systems design interview, they reach for textbook answers: they recite CAP theorem, draw standard three-tier architectures, and describe auto-scaling in generic terms. They're correct, but interchangeable. Nothing in their answer tells the interviewer that this specific person has solved hard resource allocation problems at scale.

You have something they don't: you've actually built and deployed optimization models that allocate scarce resources across a complex network under uncertainty, cost constraints, and service level requirements. That's literally the job description for this role.

The key realization is that Apple's iCloud capacity planning team doesn't need another person who can recite distributed systems theory. They need someone who can formulate a resource allocation problem as a mixed-integer program, build hierarchical forecasts that feed it, and deploy the system on cloud infrastructure. Your Walmart experience вҖ” routing imports through ports, IDCs, and RDCs; building hierarchical demand forecasts at SKU × store granularity; solving MIPs with CPLEX вҖ” maps almost one-to-one onto what this team does daily.

The Core Insight

The problem Apple is solving is: "Given uncertain future demand for cloud resources across a global network of data centers, how do we decide what to provision, where, and when вҖ” minimizing cost while guaranteeing service levels?" Replace "cloud resources" with "inventory" and "data centers" with "distribution centers" and you get: "Given uncertain future demand for products across a network of DCs, how do we decide what to stock, where, and when вҖ” minimizing cost while guaranteeing fill rates?" You've solved this. You just need to speak their language.

Why Regions and Multi-Region Design Matter

The interviewer is specifically asking about a system that works "across regions and clusters." Here are the four reasons multi-region design is essential for iCloud вҖ” understand all four, because any could come up as a follow-up question.

1

Latency

Physics is non-negotiable. Light takes ~67ms to travel from New York to London through fiber. A user in Tokyo accessing a server in Virginia will experience at minimum ~150ms of network delay. Multiply by several round trips for a typical API call and iCloud feels sluggish. Solution: serve users from the nearest region. Your provisioning engine must place capacity where users are.

2

Data Sovereignty & Compliance

The EU's GDPR requires that personal data of EU residents can be stored and processed only in compliant locations. China has similar data localization laws. Your optimization model must include hard constraints that prevent certain users' data from being placed in or migrated to non-compliant regions. This is a constraint in the MIP, not a soft objective.

3

Fault Tolerance / Disaster Recovery

If all of iCloud's infrastructure were in a single building and that building lost power, every Apple device user on Earth would be affected. Multi-region design means that if US-West experiences a major outage (earthquake, power grid failure), US-East and Europe can absorb the traffic. This is the "N+1 across regions" constraint: every region must have a designated overflow partner with pre-provisioned headroom.

4

Cost Optimization

Power costs, land costs, labor costs, and tax incentives vary dramatically across geographies. A data center in Oregon (cheap hydroelectric power) costs significantly less to operate than one in central London. Your MIP should include per-region cost coefficients. This is identical to how your Walmart model had per-DC operating costs influencing import routing.

Apple-Specific Note вҖ” Sustainability

The job description explicitly mentions "environmental sustainability." Apple runs all its data centers on 100% renewable energy and publishes annual environmental progress reports. In your interview, add a sustainability dimension to your optimization: include a carbon intensity coefficient per region in the MIP's cost function, or add a constraint that prioritizes regions powered by renewables. This shows you've read the JD carefully and understand Apple's values.

Distributed Systems Core Concepts

You need to be conversational вҖ” not expert-level вҖ” in about 15 distributed systems concepts. This section covers the ones most likely to come up, with explanations grounded in analogies to systems you already understand.

Scaling and Load Balancing

Horizontal vs. Vertical Scaling

There are two ways to handle more load. Vertical scaling means making a single machine bigger вҖ” more CPU cores, more RAM, faster storage. This is simple but has a hard ceiling (you can't buy a CPU with a million cores) and creates a single point of failure. Horizontal scaling means adding more machines and distributing the work across them. This is how cloud-scale systems work.

Nearly everything in your iCloud design should be horizontally scalable. The forecasting service, the optimization solver, the data pipeline, the monitoring system вҖ” all should be designed so that adding more instances increases throughput. The main exception is the MIP solver itself, which is inherently hard to parallelize (though you can decompose the problem by region).

Bridge to Your Experience

You've done this implicitly. Your Walmart import model routes through multiple DCs (horizontal) rather than building one giant warehouse (vertical). Your billion-scale graph work at PNNL used Spark to distribute computation across a cluster вҖ” that's horizontal scaling of data processing.

Load Balancing

When you have multiple servers (or clusters, or regions), a load balancer decides which one handles each incoming request. This is the traffic cop of distributed systems.

StrategyHow It WorksWhen to Use
Round Robin Requests go to servers 1, 2, 3, 1, 2, 3, ... Simple, works when all servers are equal and all requests are similar
Least Connections Send to whichever server currently has the fewest active requests When requests have varying processing times
Geographic / Latency-based Route users to the nearest region (DNS-based, using anycast) Global services like iCloud вҖ” users in Tokyo hit APAC, users in London hit Europe
Weighted Assign weights to servers/regions (e.g., US-West gets 40%, US-East gets 35%, Europe gets 25%) When regions have different capacities. Your provisioning engine adjusts these weights.
Consistent Hashing Hash the user ID to determine which server handles them вҖ” the same user always goes to the same server When you need session affinity or want to optimize caching (user's data is already cached on that server)

For iCloud, you'd use a combination: geographic routing at the region level (DNS-based), weighted round-robin at the cluster level (your optimizer sets the weights based on available capacity), and consistent hashing at the service level (so a user's data is always cached on the same set of nodes).

Data Management: Replication and Sharding

Replication

Replication means keeping copies of data on multiple machines (or in multiple regions). This serves two purposes: availability (if one copy is destroyed, others survive) and performance (serve reads from whichever copy is closest to the user).

There are two main architectures:

Leader-Follower (Primary-Replica)

One node is the "leader" and accepts all writes. It then replicates changes to follower nodes that serve read requests. This is simpler and guarantees write consistency, but the leader is a bottleneck and a single point of failure (mitigated by automatic leader election if the leader fails).

For iCloud: Good for services where writes happen in one region and reads happen globally (e.g., user updates their iCloud settings in the US, and the change propagates to all regions).

Multi-Leader (Multi-Primary)

Multiple nodes accept writes independently, then sync with each other. More complex because of potential write conflicts (user edits a document on both their phone and laptop simultaneously). Requires conflict resolution strategies (last-writer-wins, merge, or custom resolution).

For iCloud: Necessary for services like iCloud Drive and Notes where users in different regions might edit the same data from different devices simultaneously.

Sharding (Partitioning)

When you have more data than fits on a single machine (iCloud stores billions of users' photos вҖ” that's exabytes of data), you split the data across many machines. This is sharding.

The critical decision is the shard key вҖ” how you decide which machine holds which data:

  • By user ID: All of User #12345's data lives on Shard C. Simple, ensures all of one user's data is co-located. This is the most common approach for consumer services like iCloud.
  • By region: All EU users' data lives on EU shards. This naturally satisfies data sovereignty requirements.
  • By service: All Photos data on one set of shards, all Mail data on another. Good for services with very different storage profiles.

In practice, iCloud likely uses a combination: shard by region first (for sovereignty and latency), then by user ID within each region (for load distribution).

The Hot Shard Problem

If one shard gets disproportionately more traffic (a celebrity with millions of iCloud-shared photos, or a corporate account with massive data volumes), that shard becomes a bottleneck. This is the cloud equivalent of a single store receiving disproportionate demand. Solutions include: re-sharding (splitting the hot shard into smaller pieces), request rate limiting, or caching popular content on a CDN so requests never reach the origin shard.

Redundancy Patterns

N+1 Redundancy

If you need N servers to handle peak load, provision N+1. If any single server fails, the remaining N can still handle the full load. This is the minimum viable redundancy and is standard for most services.

MIP translation: \(\text{capacity}[c] \geq \text{peak\_demand}[c] + 1\)

N+2 or 2N Redundancy

For critical services, provision even more headroom. 2N means you double everything вҖ” full mirroring. This is expensive but provides very high availability.

When to use: Core iCloud services like Backup and Photos storage. Mention in the interview that the redundancy level is a parameter, not a constant вҖ” it varies by service criticality.

Failure Modes and Blast Radius

Blast radius is how much of the system is affected when something fails. Good architecture minimizes blast radius by isolating failures:

  • A single server fails вҶ’ only requests on that server are affected (seconds to recover via load balancer health checks)
  • A rack fails вҶ’ all servers in that rack go down, but other racks in the cluster absorb the load
  • An AZ fails вҶ’ all clusters in that AZ go down, but other AZs in the region handle the traffic
  • A region fails вҶ’ all AZs in that region go down, other regions must absorb (this is the worst case your system must handle)

Your provisioning engine's headroom must account for each failure mode. The standard is to survive the loss of any single AZ without SLA violation вҖ” this is the "fault tolerance constraint" in your MIP.

CAP Theorem, SLAs, and Consistency

CAP Theorem вҖ” Keep It Simple

The CAP theorem says that in the presence of a network partition (network split between data centers), a distributed system must choose between:

  • Consistency: Every read receives the most recent write. All users see the same version of the data.
  • Availability: Every request receives a response, even if it might not be the most recent version.

You can't have both during a partition. For iCloud:

Choose Availability (AP)

iCloud Photos, iCloud Drive, Notes вҖ” it's better for users to see a slightly stale version of their photos than to get an error. Resolve conflicts later (eventual consistency).

Choose Consistency (CP)

iCloud Keychain (passwords), billing/payment state, account authentication вҖ” you must never serve stale or conflicting data. Better to return an error than serve wrong passwords.

How to Discuss CAP in the Interview

Don't lecture about the theorem. Just say: "Different iCloud services have different consistency requirements. Photo storage can tolerate eventual consistency вҖ” I'd use an AP-oriented design where all regions can accept writes independently and sync asynchronously. But account authentication needs strong consistency вҖ” that's a CP design where writes go through a single leader and replicas might be briefly unavailable during a partition. My provisioning engine needs to account for this because CP services require more compute headroom for the consensus protocol overhead."

SLAs, SLOs, and SLIs

These three terms form the reliability framework for cloud services. They come up constantly in system design interviews.

SLI

Service Level Indicator вҖ” the actual metric you measure. Examples: "p99 latency for iCloud Photos API is 180ms" or "error rate for iCloud Backup is 0.02%." These are the signals your monitoring layer collects and your forecasting system might predict.

SLO

Service Level Objective вҖ” the internal target for your SLI. "We target p99 latency < 200ms for Photos" or "We target 99.95% availability for Backup." SLOs are set tighter than the external SLA to give you a buffer. This is exactly like setting an internal fill rate target of 98% when your customer-facing promise is 95%.

SLA

Service Level Agreement вҖ” the external, contractual promise. If Apple promises iCloud has 99.9% uptime and they miss it, there might be service credits or reputational damage. Your provisioning engine's job is to ensure SLOs are met, which in turn ensures the SLA is never violated.

Bridge to Your Experience

At Walmart, your equivalent was: SLI = actual measured fill rate, SLO = internal target fill rate (higher than customer promise), SLA = customer-facing service promise. Your eCommerce KPI forecasting work вҖ” building predictive models for fulfillment metrics with SHAP interpretability вҖ” is functionally identical to building SLI prediction models for cloud services.

Infrastructure Building Blocks вҖ” The Components on Your Whiteboard

When you draw the architecture diagram, you'll reference specific infrastructure components by name. You don't need to be an expert on any of these, but you need to know what each one does, why you chose it, and where it sits in the architecture. Here are the seven components most likely to appear on your whiteboard, organized by where they show up in the data flow.

Message Queues вҖ” Kafka

Apache Kafka

What it is: A distributed event streaming platform. Think of it as a durable, ordered, high-throughput pipe that decouples data producers (services emitting metrics) from data consumers (your forecasting pipeline, anomaly detectors, dashboards).

Why Kafka and not a simple queue: Kafka retains messages for a configurable period (days or weeks), so multiple consumers can read the same stream independently, at their own pace. Your short-term forecaster reads the same Kafka topic as your anomaly detector вҖ” they don't interfere with each other. It also handles backpressure gracefully: if your Flink consumer falls behind, messages are buffered in Kafka rather than dropped.

Where it sits in your architecture: Between the data sources and the data pipeline layer. Every infrastructure metric (CPU, memory, storage, network per node, collected every 15 seconds) is published to a Kafka topic. Flink reads from Kafka for real-time processing; Spark reads from Kafka (or a downstream data lake) for batch processing.

Key terms to know: Topics (named channels вҖ” one per metric type or service), partitions (parallelism within a topic вҖ” partition by cluster ID for locality), consumer groups (each group independently reads the full stream вҖ” your forecaster and your anomaly detector are separate consumer groups).

If asked "why not RabbitMQ?": RabbitMQ is a traditional message broker that deletes messages once consumed. Kafka retains them, enabling replay (useful for retraining models on historical data) and multiple independent consumers. At iCloud's scale (millions of metric data points per second), Kafka's throughput and durability are essential.

Stream Processing вҖ” Apache Flink

Apache Flink

What it is: A distributed stream processing engine that reads from Kafka and computes real-time aggregations, transformations, and detections with exactly-once processing guarantees.

Where it sits: In the streaming path of the data pipeline layer. Flink reads raw metrics from Kafka, computes rolling aggregates (5-minute average CPU per cluster, hourly request rate per service), detects anomalies (sudden spike in error rate), and writes processed features to the feature store for the short-term forecaster.

Why it matters for your system: The short-term forecaster needs up-to-date features (e.g., "what was the average CPU across US-West clusters in the last 5 minutes?"). Flink computes these in real-time with low latency (sub-second). Without it, you'd have to wait for batch processing вҖ” too slow for auto-scaling decisions.

If asked "why not Spark Streaming?": Spark Streaming uses micro-batches (process every few seconds), while Flink is true event-at-a-time processing with lower latency. For auto-scaling decisions where seconds matter, Flink is the better fit. Spark is still used for the batch path (training data prep, historical aggregations).

Caching вҖ” Redis

Redis

What it is: An in-memory key-value store. Data lives in RAM, so reads are sub-millisecond вҖ” orders of magnitude faster than hitting a database on disk.

Where it sits in your architecture: Two places:

  • Feature store cache: The feature store has a Redis layer for online serving. When the short-term forecaster needs the latest features for a cluster, it reads from Redis (sub-millisecond) rather than querying the underlying database (10-100ms). Features are written to Redis by Flink as they're computed.
  • Forecast result cache: The auto-scaler queries the forecaster frequently ("what's the predicted demand for US-West Photos in the next hour?"). Rather than re-running inference every time, the latest forecast is cached in Redis with a short TTL (e.g., 5 minutes). Multiple auto-scaler instances across clusters can read the same cached forecast.

Redis vs. Memcached: Both are in-memory caches. Redis supports richer data structures (sorted sets, hashes, lists) and persistence options. Memcached is simpler and slightly faster for pure key-value lookups. For your system, Redis is the better choice because you may want to store structured feature vectors, not just flat values. But if the interviewer asks, either works вҖ” the choice is not critical to the design.

If asked "what if Redis goes down?": The feature store has a fallback path вҖ” read from the underlying database (slower but functional). The system degrades gracefully: auto-scaling decisions are slightly delayed, not lost. This is a good example of the availability-over-consistency principle for non-critical reads.

Databases вҖ” Time-Series DB and Relational DB

Time-Series Database (Prometheus / InfluxDB / Apple internal)

What it is: A database optimized for storing and querying time-stamped metric data. Think: "give me the CPU utilization of Cluster A for every 15-second interval over the last 30 days."

Where it sits: This is the persistent storage behind your metrics collection. Raw infrastructure metrics flow from the nodes вҶ’ Kafka вҶ’ time-series DB. The batch pipeline reads historical data from here for training the medium and long-term forecasters.

Why not a regular database? Time-series data has special access patterns: writes are always append-only (new timestamps), reads are almost always range queries over time, and old data can be downsampled (keep 15-second granularity for the last week, 5-minute granularity for the last year). Time-series databases are optimized for exactly this: columnar storage for compression, time-based indexing, and automatic downsampling/retention policies.

Sharding strategy: Shard by cluster ID or region. Each region's metrics stay on local shards, which aligns with data locality and reduces cross-region query traffic.

Relational Database (PostgreSQL / MySQL)

What it is: A traditional SQL database for storing structured configuration and state data.

Where it sits: Stores the non-time-series data your system needs: the MIP's constraint parameters (per-region costs, power limits, SLA targets), the optimizer's output (current target allocation per cluster), the current state of provisioned infrastructure, and the reconciliation parameters. This is a much smaller dataset вҖ” thousands of rows, not billions of time-series points.

Why mention it: It shows the interviewer you recognize that not everything is a time-series problem. The optimizer's configuration and decision output needs ACID guarantees (you don't want a half-written allocation plan), which a relational DB provides.

Load Balancers

Load Balancers (L4 / L7)

Already covered in detail in the Scaling section above, but here's how they fit in the architecture:

  • Global load balancer (DNS-based / Anycast): Sits in front of all regions. Routes users to the nearest healthy region. Your optimizer adjusts region weights via the execution layer. This is where geographic routing, failover, and traffic shifting happen.
  • Regional load balancer (L7 вҖ” HTTP-aware): Within each region, distributes requests across clusters and service instances. Performs health checks (if a pod fails, stop sending traffic to it). Implements the drain-before-kill pattern for graceful scale-down.
  • Service mesh (optional depth): For inter-service communication (e.g., the Photos API calling the Storage backend), a service mesh like Istio or Envoy provides load balancing, retry logic, and circuit breaking between microservices. Mention only if asked.

Connection to your optimizer: The optimizer's load balancing weights \(w_{s,r,t}\) are applied by updating the global load balancer's routing rules. This is one of the fastest provisioning actions (seconds to propagate) as covered in the Decision Propagation section.

Orchestration вҖ” Kubernetes

Kubernetes (K8s)

What it is: A container orchestration platform that manages the lifecycle of your services вҖ” deploying, scaling, and healing containers across a cluster of machines.

Key concepts (just enough for the interview):

  • Pod: The smallest deployable unit вҖ” one or more containers running together. Your forecasting service, your auto-scaler, your anomaly detector вҖ” each runs as pods.
  • Deployment: Manages a set of identical pods. "Run 10 replicas of the short-term-forecaster pod." Kubernetes ensures exactly 10 are always running; if one crashes, it's replaced automatically.
  • Horizontal Pod Autoscaler (HPA): Watches a metric (CPU, custom metric from your forecaster) and adjusts the number of pod replicas. This is the reactive auto-scaling mechanism discussed in Section 5.
  • Node: A physical or virtual machine in the cluster. Pods are scheduled onto nodes. When all nodes are full, the Cluster Autoscaler provisions new nodes (VMs).
  • Namespace / Resource Quotas: Isolate resources per service or team. You can set limits: "the Photos service can use at most 5000 CPU cores in this cluster." This ties directly into your optimizer's per-service capacity allocation.

Where it sits: The execution and orchestration layer. When the auto-scaler or capacity planner says "add 200 pods to Cluster A," Kubernetes is the system that actually creates those pods, schedules them onto nodes, and connects them to the load balancer.

CDN вҖ” Content Delivery Network

CDN (Content Delivery Network)

What it is: A globally distributed network of edge servers that cache and serve content close to users. For iCloud, the CDN handles static and semi-static content (photo thumbnails, cached documents, public assets) so that requests never need to reach the origin data center.

Where it sits: In front of everything вҖ” between the user's device and your regional data centers. The CDN is the first layer that absorbs demand. A well-configured CDN can handle 60-80% of read traffic without touching origin servers, dramatically reducing the compute and network demand that your forecaster needs to predict.

Why it matters for your system: Your forecasting model should account for the CDN's cache hit rate. If the CDN absorbs 70% of Photos read traffic, your origin cluster only sees 30%. A change in CDN configuration (e.g., shorter TTLs, cache purge) can suddenly increase origin traffic вҖ” the forecaster needs this as an input signal. The optimizer should also consider CDN capacity (edge node count, cache storage per edge location) as a provisioning variable.

Hierarchical Forecasting вҖ” Your Superpower

This is where you have a decisive advantage over other candidates. Most people who come from a pure distributed systems background will suggest "just forecast per cluster." You know from Walmart that this doesn't work вҖ” you need hierarchical forecasting with reconciliation.

The Hierarchy for iCloud

Level 0 (Top): Global total demand for each resource type в”Ӯ Level 1: Per-Region demand в”Ӯ (US-West, US-East, Europe, APAC, ...) в”Ӯ Level 2: Per-AZ demand в”Ӯ (US-West-AZ1, US-West-AZ2, ...) в”Ӯ Level 3: Per-Cluster demand в”Ӯ (US-West-AZ1-Cluster-A, ...) в”Ӯ Level 4 (Bottom): Per-Service Г— Per-Cluster demand (Photos on US-West-AZ1-Cluster-A, Backup on US-West-AZ1-Cluster-A, ...)

Why You Can't Just Forecast at One Level

Problem with bottom-up only

Forecasting at the bottom level (service × cluster) gives granular detail but has high variance вҖ” small clusters don't have enough data for stable forecasts. The noise at the bottom creates incoherent totals when you sum up. This is the same reason you can't forecast individual SKU × store and expect the national total to make sense.

Problem with top-down only

Forecasting at the top level (global total) gives stable predictions but loses all geographic and service-level nuance. You can't make cluster-level provisioning decisions from a global number. And proportionally splitting a global forecast ignores that different regions grow at different rates.

The Solution: Reconciliation

Forecast at every level of the hierarchy independently, then reconcile so that forecasts are coherent (the parts sum to the whole). You did exactly this at Walmart вҖ” building forecasts at SKU × store granularity for short/medium horizon, then aggregating and reconciling to lane/DC signals for long-term capacity planning.

The three reconciliation approaches:

1

Top-Down

Forecast the global total, then allocate proportionally to regions вҶ’ AZs вҶ’ clusters based on historical proportions. Simple but assumes the mix stays stable вҖ” which breaks during regional growth spurts or new market launches.

2

Bottom-Up

Forecast each bottom-level node (service × cluster), then sum up to get higher-level totals. Captures local patterns well but is noisy and may produce unrealistic global totals.

3

Optimal Reconciliation (MinT / WLS)

Forecast at all levels, then use a reconciliation method (like minimum trace reconciliation) to find the set of coherent forecasts that minimizes overall forecast error. This is the state-of-the-art approach and the one you should propose in the interview. You used this at Walmart вҖ” say so.

Always mention that your forecasts produce prediction intervals, not just point estimates:

Resource Provisioning & Optimization

This section is where your MIP expertise shines. You'll learn to frame the iCloud capacity allocation problem as a formal optimization problem вҖ” using the same techniques you applied at Walmart with CPLEX, but with cloud-specific constraints and objectives.

The MIP Formulation

In the interview, being able to write out a clean mathematical formulation вҖ” or at least describe it clearly вҖ” will set you apart from candidates who only speak at a conceptual level. Here's the complete formulation:

Sets and Indices

$$\begin{aligned} \mathcal{R} &= \text{set of regions} & (r \in \mathcal{R}) \\[4pt] \mathcal{C} &= \text{set of clusters} & (c \in \mathcal{C},\ c \subset r) \\[4pt] \mathcal{S} &= \text{set of services} & (s \in \mathcal{S}: \text{Photos, Backup, Mail, } \ldots) \\[4pt] \mathcal{K} &= \text{set of resource types} & (k \in \mathcal{K}: \text{CPU, Storage, RAM, Network}) \\[4pt] \mathcal{T} &= \text{set of time periods} & (t \in \mathcal{T}: \text{quarters or hours}) \end{aligned}$$

Decision Variables

$$x_{r,k,t} \in \mathbb{Z}^+ \quad \text{вҖ” units of resource } k \text{ to provision in region } r \text{ at time } t$$ Integer for hardware purchases, continuous for VM allocation $$y_{r,t} \in \{0, 1\} \quad \text{вҖ” 1 if we expand capacity in region } r \text{ at time } t$$ Binary; triggers fixed cost of expansion $$w_{s,r,t} \in [0, 1] \quad \text{вҖ” fraction of traffic for service } s \text{ routed to region } r \text{ at time } t$$ Load balancing weights, continuous

Objective Function

Minimize $$\underbrace{\sum_{r \in \mathcal{R}} \sum_{k \in \mathcal{K}} \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}} c_{r,k} \cdot x_{r,k,t}}_{\text{hardware / VM cost}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sum_{r \in \mathcal{R}} \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}} f_r \cdot y_{r,t}}_{\text{expansion fixed costs}}$$ $$+\; \underbrace{\sum_{r \in \mathcal{R}} \sum_{k \in \mathcal{K}} \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}} p_r \cdot x_{r,k,t}}_{\text{operating costs (power, cooling)}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sum_{r \in \mathcal{R}} \gamma_r \cdot E_r}_{\text{sustainability penalty}}$$ $$+\; \underbrace{\sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}} \sum_{r \in \mathcal{R}} \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}} M_s \cdot v_{s,r,t}}_{\text{SLA violation penalty (big-}M\text{)}}$$

Constraints

1. Demand Satisfaction For each service \(s\), region \(r\), resource \(k\), time \(t\): $$\text{capacity}_{r,k,t} \;\geq\; \hat{d}^{\,P90}_{s,r,k,t} \;+\; h_s$$ Available capacity must exceed the P90 demand forecast plus headroom. Cloud equivalent of: \(\text{inventory} \geq \text{demand} + \text{safety stock}\).
2. SLA / Availability For each service \(s\), region \(r\): $$A_{s,r} \;\geq\; A^{\text{target}}_s$$ Enforced via redundancy вҖ” enough spare capacity to survive any single-AZ loss.
3. Fault Tolerance вҖ” AZ Loss For each region \(r\), each AZ \(a \in r\), resource \(k\): $$\sum_{c \,\in\, \mathcal{C}_r \setminus \mathcal{C}_a} \text{capacity}_{c,k} \;\geq\; d_{r,k}$$ Even if AZ \(a\) fails completely, the remaining AZs can handle the full load.
4. Data Sovereignty For each user group \(g\) with a regional requirement: $$\text{loc}(g) \;\in\; \mathcal{R}^{\text{allowed}}_g$$ EU user data must reside in EU or approved regions. Hard constraint вҖ” no relaxation.
5. Procurement Lead Time For hardware purchases of resource type \(k\): $$x_{r,k,t} \;\text{must be decided at or before}\; t - L_k$$ Server procurement takes \(L_k \approx 8\text{вҖ“}16\) weeks. Decisions can't be retroactive.
6. Budget Per time period \(t\): $$\sum_{r \in \mathcal{R}} \sum_{k \in \mathcal{K}} c_{r,k} \cdot x_{r,k,t} \;\leq\; B_t$$ Annual CapEx budget is finite.
7. Rack / Power Constraints Per cluster \(c\), time \(t\): $$\sum_{k \in \mathcal{K}} \pi_k \cdot x_{c,k,t} \;\leq\; P^{\max}_c$$ Each cluster has a physical power limit \(P^{\max}_c\). Cannot exceed electrical capacity.
8. Load Balancing For each service \(s\), time \(t\): $$\sum_{r \in \mathcal{R}} w_{s,r,t} = 1 \qquad \text{and} \qquad w_{s,r,t} \geq 0 \;\;\forall\, r$$ Traffic weights must sum to 1 across regions.
9. Migration Rate Limits For storage moves between regions \(r_1\) and \(r_2\), time \(t\): $$m_{r_1 \to r_2, t} \;\leq\; \beta_{r_1, r_2}$$ Network capacity and cost limit how fast you can rebalance storage across regions.
Bridge to Your Walmart MIP

This formulation is structurally identical to your Walmart import routing model. In that model: decision variables = flow of goods through ports вҶ’ IDCs вҶ’ RDCs; objective = minimize transportation cost; constraints = demand satisfaction at stores, capacity limits at DCs, port throughput limits, and service level requirements. The mathematical structure is the same вҖ” only the nouns change. In the interview, say this explicitly.

Strategic Layer вҖ” Quarterly Capacity Planning

The strategic optimization runs quarterly (or monthly) and makes the big, expensive, hard-to-reverse decisions. This is the MIP described above.

What Decisions It Makes

  • Hardware procurement: How many servers of each type to order for each region. These have 8-16 week lead times, so decisions must be made months ahead of need.
  • Region expansion: Should we add capacity in a new region (e.g., build infrastructure in India or Brazil)? Binary decision with high fixed cost.
  • Capacity rebalancing: Should we shift storage from an over-provisioned region to an under-provisioned one? Constrained by migration bandwidth.
  • Retirement scheduling: When to decommission aging hardware вҖ” balancing replacement cost against maintenance cost and failure risk.

Solving the MIP

For a problem of this scale (dozens of regions, hundreds of clusters, multiple resource types, multiple time periods), the MIP will have thousands to tens of thousands of variables and constraints. This is well within the capability of commercial solvers like CPLEX or Gurobi, which you've used at Walmart.

Practical tips to mention in the interview:

  • Decomposition by region: If the full MIP is too large, decompose it into per-region subproblems linked by inter-region constraints (Benders decomposition or Lagrangian relaxation). This is a technique used in large-scale supply chain optimization.
  • Scenario-based stochastic optimization: Instead of using a single P90 demand forecast, solve the MIP under multiple demand scenarios (optimistic, baseline, pessimistic) and choose the solution that performs best across scenarios. This is robust optimization вҖ” mention it to show sophistication.
  • Rolling horizon: Solve the MIP quarterly, but only commit to the first quarter's decisions. Re-solve next quarter with updated forecasts. This hedges against long-term forecast uncertainty.

LP Relaxation вҖ” When MIPs Are Too Slow

Here's a practical reality the interviewer may probe: a full MIP with integer variables can take minutes to hours to solve, depending on problem size. That's fine for quarterly strategic planning, but what about decisions at a faster cadence вҖ” say, hourly or daily rebalancing of workloads across clusters? You can't wait 45 minutes for CPLEX to converge when you need an answer in seconds.

What Is LP Relaxation?

LP relaxation drops the integrality constraints from the MIP, allowing integer and binary variables to take continuous (fractional) values. The resulting Linear Program (LP) is dramatically faster to solve вҖ” typically polynomial time via simplex or interior-point methods, versus NP-hard for the MIP.

MIP вҶ’ LP Relaxation Original MIP integrality constraints: $$x_{r,k,t} \in \mathbb{Z}^+ \qquad y_{r,t} \in \{0, 1\}$$ Relaxed LP: $$x_{r,k,t} \geq 0 \quad (x \in \mathbb{R}) \qquad 0 \leq y_{r,t} \leq 1 \quad (y \in \mathbb{R})$$ The LP optimal value provides a lower bound on the MIP optimal value: $$z^*_{\text{LP}} \;\leq\; z^*_{\text{MIP}}$$

When LP Relaxation Is Appropriate

The key insight is that integrality matters most when decision units are large and lumpy. It matters least when units are small relative to the total.

Weekly / Monthly Allocate VM pools across regions MIP or LP + rounding VMs are somewhat granular but you're allocating hundreds. LP solution rounded to nearest integer is near-optimal. MIP feasible if problem is decomposed by region.

Rounding Strategies After LP Relaxation

When the LP produces fractional solutions for variables that should be integer, you need a rounding strategy. This is well-studied in OR:

1

Simple Rounding (Ceiling)

Round every fractional variable up: \(\lceil x^*_{\text{LP}} \rceil\). This guarantees feasibility (all demand constraints remain satisfied since you're provisioning more, not less) but slightly increases cost. For capacity provisioning this is the natural choice вҖ” you'd rather have one extra pod than one too few.

2

Randomized Rounding

Round each fractional variable probabilistically: \(x^*_{\text{LP}} = 47.7\) rounds to 48 with probability 0.7 and to 47 with probability 0.3. Provides provable approximation guarantees in expectation. More relevant for theoretical analysis than production use.

3

LP-Guided MIP (Warm Start)

Use the LP relaxation solution as a warm start for the MIP solver. The LP solution gives the solver a strong initial feasible region, dramatically reducing branch-and-bound search time. This is the best of both worlds: you get exact integer solutions, but the solver converges much faster. CPLEX and Gurobi both support warm starts natively.

The Optimality Gap вҖ” How Good Is the LP Solution?

The LP relaxation gives you a lower bound \(z^*_{\text{LP}}\) and the rounded solution gives a feasible upper bound \(z^{\text{rounded}}\). The optimality gap tells you how far from optimal your practical solution is:

$$\text{gap} = \frac{z^{\text{rounded}} - z^*_{\text{LP}}}{z^*_{\text{LP}}} \times 100\%$$

In practice, for capacity allocation problems with many small units (pods, containers), this gap is often <1%. That means the LP + ceiling approach gives you a solution within 1% of the true MIP optimum вҖ” at a fraction of the solve time. Mention this in the interview to show you understand the practical tradeoff between solution quality and computational budget.

Bridge to Your Experience

At Walmart, your CPLEX models likely hit solve-time limits on the largest instances. You've seen this tradeoff firsthand: sometimes you accept a solution with a 2% optimality gap because waiting for the provably optimal solution would miss the planning deadline. The same principle applies here вҖ” at hourly cadence, a near-optimal LP solution delivered in 5 seconds beats an optimal MIP solution delivered in 30 minutes.

Tactical Layer вҖ” Real-Time Auto-Scaling

The tactical layer operates within the capacity envelope set by the strategic MIP. It makes minute-to-minute decisions about how to use existing resources. Think of it as daily replenishment operating within the inventory bought by the quarterly purchasing plan.

Kubernetes Horizontal Pod Autoscaler (HPA) вҖ” The Basics

In a Kubernetes-based infrastructure (which most modern cloud services use), workloads run in pods (containers). The Horizontal Pod Autoscaler watches a metric (typically CPU utilization) and automatically adds or removes pods:

Reactive Auto-Scaling Rule $$\text{IF} \quad \bar{u}^{\text{CPU}}_{\Delta t=5\text{min}} > 0.75 \implies n_{\text{pods}} \leftarrow n_{\text{pods}} + 2$$ $$\text{IF} \quad \bar{u}^{\text{CPU}}_{\Delta t=10\text{min}} < 0.30 \implies n_{\text{pods}} \leftarrow n_{\text{pods}} - 1$$ This is the baseline. It works, but it's reactive вҖ” by the time CPU hits 75%, users may already be experiencing higher latency.

Predictive Auto-Scaling вҖ” Your Value Add

This is where your forecasting expertise directly improves the system. Instead of only reacting to current metrics, the auto-scaler uses short-term forecasts to pre-scale before demand arrives.

Predictive Auto-Scaling (Your Contribution) $$\hat{d}_{t+\Delta} = f\!\left(\mathbf{x}_t,\; \mathbf{x}_{t-1},\; \ldots,\; \text{calendar features}\right) \quad \text{(short-term forecaster)}$$ $$\text{IF} \quad \hat{d}_{t+\Delta} > 0.7 \cdot \text{capacity}_t \implies \text{pre-scale NOW}$$ $$\text{IF event} \in \text{calendar}(t+\Delta) \implies \hat{d}_{t+\Delta} \leftarrow \hat{d}_{t+\Delta} \cdot \lambda_{\text{event}}$$ where \(\lambda_{\text{event}}\) is a historical event multiplier (e.g., iPhone launch вҶ’ 3.2x iCloud Backup demand). Resources are ready BEFORE users arrive. Same concept as pre-positioning inventory before a known demand surge.

The hybrid approach вҖ” predictive scaling as the primary mechanism, reactive scaling as the fallback вҖ” is the state of the art. Propose this in the interview and explain that the predictive component uses the same short-term forecaster from Section 4.

Cross-Region Traffic Shifting

When one region is approaching capacity limits and another has headroom, the tactical layer can shift traffic between regions by adjusting DNS weights or load balancer configuration. This is the cloud equivalent of lateral transfers between warehouses.

Constraints on traffic shifting:

  • Latency impact: Shifting a Tokyo user to US-West adds ~100ms latency вҖ” acceptable for batch operations, unacceptable for real-time services.
  • Data locality: The user's data might be stored in APAC; serving them from US-West requires cross-region data access, which is slow and expensive.
  • Data sovereignty: Can't shift EU user traffic to non-EU regions if data sovereignty applies.

Multi-Resource Co-Optimization

This is a nuance that will impress the interviewer: resources are not independent. You can't provision CPU without also provisioning the memory and network bandwidth to support it. The MIP handles this via coupling constraints.

The Coupling Problem

Imagine you forecast that a cluster needs 1000 additional CPU cores. You provision them. But you forgot that each CPU core also needs ~4GB of RAM, ~100GB of storage, and ~1Gbps of network capacity for the workloads that will run on it. If the cluster doesn't have enough RAM, the extra CPUs sit idle вҖ” stranded capacity.

In supply chain terms: it's like shipping cereal to a store but not shipping milk. They're consumed together. Your MIP must enforce resource ratio constraints that ensure complementary resources are provisioned together.

Resource Coupling Constraints For each cluster \(c\), time period \(t\): $$\begin{aligned} x^{\text{RAM}}_{c,t} &\;\geq\; x^{\text{CPU}}_{c,t} \cdot \alpha^{\text{RAM}}_{\,\mu(c)} \\[6pt] x^{\text{storage}}_{c,t} &\;\geq\; x^{\text{CPU}}_{c,t} \cdot \alpha^{\text{storage}}_{\,\mu(c)} \\[6pt] x^{\text{net}}_{c,t} &\;\geq\; x^{\text{CPU}}_{c,t} \cdot \alpha^{\text{net}}_{\,\mu(c)} \end{aligned}$$ where \(\mu(c)\) is the service mix running on cluster \(c\), and \(\alpha\) values are the per-resource ratios. A Photos-heavy cluster has higher \(\alpha^{\text{storage}}\) than a Siri-heavy cluster.

This level of detail вҖ” recognizing that resources are coupled and the ratios depend on workload mix вҖ” is something that comes naturally from supply chain thinking (you'd never plan inventory for a burger restaurant by ordering buns without proportional patties). But many system design candidates miss it.

Decision Propagation вҖ” From Optimizer Output to Running Infrastructure

Most system design candidates stop at "the optimizer decides what to provision." But there's a critical gap between the optimizer says x and x is actually running and serving traffic. This is the execution layer вҖ” and it's where real systems get messy. If the interviewer asks "okay, so now you have the optimal allocation вҖ” what happens next?", this is what you need to cover.

The Decision Delta

The optimizer's output is a target state. The current infrastructure is the actual state. The work to be done is the delta between the two:

Decision Delta $$\Delta_{r,k,t} \;=\; x^{\text{target}}_{r,k,t} \;-\; x^{\text{current}}_{r,k,t}$$ If \(\Delta > 0\): provision (scale up). If \(\Delta < 0\): decommission (scale down). If \(\Delta = 0\): no action needed. The execution plan must translate this into a sequence of concrete operations: $$\Delta_{r,k,t} \;\longrightarrow\; \bigl\{\text{order hardware},\;\text{spin up VMs},\;\text{deploy pods},\;\text{migrate data},\;\text{update DNS}\bigr\}$$

Propagation Delays at Each Layer

Each type of provisioning action has a different latency. The optimizer must account for these delays вҖ” you can't assume decisions are instantaneous. This is directly analogous to lead times in supply chain, where different suppliers and transportation modes have different delivery times.

Spin up new VMs (within existing physical capacity) 1вҖ“5 minutes Reversible Activating reserve pallet positions in a warehouse

Implication for the Optimizer

The optimizer must be lead-time-aware. If the forecast says US-West needs 500 more servers in Q3, the procurement decision must be made in Q1 (given a 12-week lead time). But if the forecast says a cluster needs 200 more pods in 2 hours, that decision can be made now and executed in minutes. This is why the two-layer architecture (strategic MIP + tactical LP/auto-scaler) exists вҖ” they operate at different decision cadences matched to different propagation latencies.

Partial Rollouts and Transient States

In practice, provisioning doesn't happen atomically. Between the moment you start executing a delta and the moment the new state is fully live, the system is in a transient state where only part of the change has been applied. This is where things get dangerous.

Problem 1: Capacity Gaps During Scale-Up

Suppose the optimizer says: "add 300 pods to Cluster A and shift 20% of traffic from Cluster B to Cluster A." If the traffic shift happens before the pods are ready, Cluster A gets overwhelmed:

Dangerous Ordering $$\underbrace{w_{s,A} \leftarrow w_{s,A} + 0.20}_{\text{traffic arrives immediately}} \quad \text{but} \quad \underbrace{x^{\text{pods}}_{A} \leftarrow x^{\text{pods}}_{A} + 300}_{\text{pods need 2 min to start}}$$ For ~2 minutes, Cluster A is receiving 20% more traffic than it can handle вҶ’ latency spike, potential SLA violation. Safe Ordering $$x^{\text{pods}}_{A} \leftarrow x^{\text{pods}}_{A} + 300 \quad \xrightarrow{\text{wait for health checks}} \quad w_{s,A} \leftarrow w_{s,A} + 0.20$$ Always provision capacity BEFORE shifting traffic. Resources first, traffic second.

Problem 2: Data Locality Violations During Migration

If you're migrating user data from Region A to Region B (to rebalance storage), there's a period where the data is in transit вҖ” it exists in A but not yet in B, or partially in both. During this window:

  • Reads for migrating users must still hit Region A (the data isn't in B yet), even if the load balancer has already started routing those users to B
  • Writes are especially tricky вҖ” do they go to A (which is the source being drained) or B (which doesn't have the full dataset yet)?
  • If the migration fails midway, you need a rollback plan that doesn't lose data

The standard solution is a two-phase migration:

Two-Phase Data Migration Phase 1 вҖ” Replication (no traffic shift yet): $$\text{data}_{B} \leftarrow \text{copy}(\text{data}_{A}) \quad \text{while} \quad w_{s,A} = 1.0,\; w_{s,B} = 0.0$$ Phase 2 вҖ” Cutover (after replication is verified complete): $$\text{verify: } \text{data}_{B} = \text{data}_{A} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad w_{s,B} \leftarrow 1.0,\; w_{s,A} \leftarrow 0.0$$ Phase 3 вҖ” Cleanup (after cutover is stable): $$\text{TTL expires} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \text{delete}(\text{data}_{A})$$

Problem 3: Cascading Failures During Scale-Down

Scale-down is riskier than scale-up. If the optimizer says "remove 100 pods from Cluster B," and you remove them all at once, you might:

  • Kill active user sessions on those pods (requests in-flight get dropped)
  • Overwhelm remaining pods if the load balancer hasn't adjusted weights yet
  • Trigger health check failures that cause the auto-scaler to panic and scale back up, creating an oscillation loop

Graceful Rollout Strategies

Canary Rollout

Apply the delta to a small subset first (e.g., 5% of pods), observe metrics for a validation window (5вҖ“15 minutes), and only proceed to the full rollout if SLIs remain healthy. If the canary shows degradation, automatically roll back.

For provisioning changes: shift 2% of traffic to the new capacity, validate latency and error rates, then proceed in increments вҖ” 2% вҶ’ 10% вҶ’ 25% вҶ’ 50% вҶ’ 100%.

Drain-Before-Kill

Before removing a pod or node, mark it as "draining" вҖ” the load balancer stops sending new requests, but existing in-flight requests are allowed to complete. Only after the drain timeout (typically 30вҖ“60 seconds) is the pod actually terminated.

This prevents dropped requests and is standard practice in Kubernetes (terminationGracePeriodSeconds) and all major load balancers.

Rate-Limited Scale-Down

Never remove more than a fixed percentage of capacity in a single step. A common rule: remove at most 10% of pods per 5-minute window. This prevents large sudden drops that could cascade. Mathematically:

\(\left| \Delta^{-}_{c,k,t} \right| \;\leq\; 0.10 \cdot x^{\text{current}}_{c,k,t}\) per step
Rollback Automation

Every provisioning action should be paired with an automated rollback trigger. If SLIs (latency, error rate) degrade beyond a threshold within the validation window, the system automatically reverts to the previous state. No human approval needed for rollback вҖ” only for proceeding forward.

Oscillation вҖ” The Auto-Scaler's Worst Enemy

Without dampening, the following can happen: demand rises вҶ’ scaler adds pods вҶ’ demand is now over-provisioned вҶ’ scaler removes pods вҶ’ demand spikes again вҶ’ scaler adds pods ... This is oscillation (sometimes called "thrashing"), and it wastes resources and creates instability.

Solutions:

  • Cooldown periods: After a scale-up, block scale-down for a minimum window (e.g., 10 minutes). After scale-down, block further scale-down for 5 minutes.
  • Hysteresis: Scale up at 75% utilization but only scale down at 30%. The wide gap between thresholds prevents rapid flipping.
  • Smoothing: Use exponentially weighted moving averages of utilization rather than instantaneous readings. A 5-minute EWMA filters out transient spikes.

In the interview, mentioning oscillation and hysteresis shows you've thought about operational stability, not just the steady-state math.

"How do you handle an iPhone launch spike?"

Opening: "iPhone launches are known-in-advance demand surges вҖ” the cloud equivalent of Black Friday in retail. I'd handle them at three time scales."

Long-term (months before): The strategic MIP incorporates Apple's product calendar. Historical data from previous launches (iPhone 15 вҶ’ X% increase in iCloud Backup within 72 hours, Y PB of new photo storage within 30 days) gives us strong priors. The MIP pre-provisions capacity in regions where launch-day demand will be highest (typically US first, then staggered global rollout).

Medium-term (weeks before): As the launch approaches, we refine forecasts with pre-order data and marketing signals. The capacity planner ensures procurement orders placed 8-16 weeks ago are arriving and being racked on schedule. We also pre-warm caches and verify that failover paths work.

Short-term (hours/minutes): The predictive auto-scaler, trained on previous launch patterns, pre-scales services 2-4 hours before the anticipated surge. The reactive auto-scaler handles any deviation from the prediction. We also shift non-critical batch workloads (ML training, data compaction, analytics) to off-peak regions or defer them entirely, freeing capacity for user-facing traffic.

Bridge: "At Walmart, I handled structurally identical surges for holiday demand. The forecaster incorporated promotional calendars, and the MIP pre-positioned inventory weeks before peak. The math is the same."

"What if your forecast is wrong?"

Opening: "Every forecast is wrong вҖ” the question is how the system degrades gracefully when it is."

Defense 1 вҖ” Prediction intervals: We don't provision for the P50 (median) forecast. We provision for P80 or P90, which embeds statistical safety stock. The gap between P50 and P90 is our buffer. The more uncertain the forecast, the wider this interval, and the more headroom we provision. This is identical to safety stock formulas in supply chain вҖ” \(SS = z \cdot \sigma \cdot \sqrt{LT}\).

Defense 2 вҖ” Reactive auto-scaling: Even if the predictive component misses, the reactive auto-scaler kicks in when CPU exceeds 75% or error rate exceeds threshold. This adds capacity within minutes. It's not ideal (users experience briefly elevated latency during the scale-up), but it prevents outages.

Defense 3 вҖ” N+1 redundancy: The fault tolerance constraint in the MIP ensures that every region can survive the loss of an AZ. This structural headroom is always present, independent of forecast accuracy. If the forecast is wrong and demand spikes, the headroom reserved for fault tolerance acts as a buffer (though this is a last resort вҖ” you don't want to consume your fault tolerance margin for demand spikes).

Defense 4 вҖ” Feedback and retraining: The monitoring layer tracks forecast accuracy in real-time. If MAPE exceeds a threshold or persistent bias is detected, an automatic retraining job kicks off. We also do post-mortem analysis after any significant forecast miss to identify root causes and improve the model.

"How do you decide where to place capacity вҖ” US-West vs. Europe vs. Asia?"

Opening: "This is the strategic MIP's core job вҖ” it's a multi-region capacity allocation problem."

The optimization: Decision variables are how much of each resource type to provision per region. The objective minimizes total cost вҖ” which includes hardware, power (varies by region вҖ” Oregon is cheap hydroelectric, London is expensive), network transit between regions, and a carbon intensity penalty to align with Apple's sustainability goals. Constraints enforce demand satisfaction per region (based on forecast), latency requirements (users must be served from a region within X ms), data sovereignty (EU data stays in EU), fault tolerance (survive loss of any region by having overflow capacity in neighboring regions), and budget limits.

Bridge: "At Walmart, I solved a structurally identical problem вҖ” routing imports through ports, import DCs, and regional DCs to minimize cost while meeting demand at every store. The decision variables were flows through the network; constraints were port capacities, DC capacities, and service levels. The math translates directly."

Nuance: I'd add scenario-based robust optimization вҖ” solve the MIP under optimistic, baseline, and pessimistic demand scenarios and choose the solution that's feasible across all scenarios. This hedges against long-term forecast uncertainty, especially for emerging regions where historical data is sparse.

"What happens when a region goes down?"

Opening: "The system handles this at multiple layers, with automated responses at each."

Layer 1 вҖ” Immediate (seconds): Health checks from the global load balancer detect the failure. DNS or anycast routing automatically shifts traffic to the designated overflow regions. No human intervention needed. Users experience a brief increase in latency (they're now being served from a farther region) but no outage.

Layer 2 вҖ” Short-term (minutes): The auto-scaler in the surviving regions detects the traffic increase and scales up services. The short-term forecaster adjusts its predictions to account for the redistribution. Alerts fire to the on-call engineering team.

Layer 3 вҖ” Medium-term (hours-days): The capacity planner evaluates whether the surviving regions have enough headroom to sustain the extra load indefinitely or if this is consuming fault-tolerance margins that need to be restored. If needed, additional capacity is provisioned (spinning up reserved cloud instances from a third-party provider as a bridge).

Why this works: The N+1 constraint in the MIP ensures that every region's designated overflow partner has pre-provisioned headroom. This headroom is "paid for" by the MIP's cost function вҖ” it's an explicit cost we accept for the reliability guarantee. This is the same concept as safety stock in a multi-DC supply chain вҖ” each DC holds buffer inventory for itself and to absorb demand if a neighboring DC goes offline.

"How do you handle multiple resource types that need to scale together?"

Opening: "Resources are coupled вҖ” you can't add CPU without proportional memory, storage, and network. The MIP handles this with coupling constraints."

The coupling: Each workload type (service) has a resource profile вҖ” a ratio of CPU : RAM : storage : network that it requires. A Photos-heavy cluster needs ~10x more storage per CPU than a Siri-heavy cluster. The MIP includes constraints that enforce these ratios: if you provision X CPU cores on a cluster, you must also provision at least X Г— ram_ratio GB of RAM and X Г— storage_ratio TB of storage. Otherwise you get stranded capacity вҖ” CPUs sitting idle because there's no RAM to pair with them.

Bridge: "This is like a multi-product supply chain where products are consumed together. You don't ship burger buns to a restaurant without proportional patties. At Walmart, our import model routed complementary product categories together through the same DC lanes to ensure balanced fulfillment. The MIP coupling constraints are the formal version of this."

Physical constraints: Beyond ratios, there are physical limits. Each rack has a power budget (say, 20kW). Each server configuration has a fixed power draw. The MIP must respect these: total power draw of all equipment in a rack cannot exceed the rack's power limit. Similarly, each data center has a total cooling capacity and network uplink bandwidth that caps what you can provision there, regardless of demand.

"How would you evaluate whether this system is working well?"

Opening: "I'd track three categories of metrics: forecast quality, provisioning efficiency, and business outcomes."

Forecast quality: MAPE and bias (are we systematically over- or under-predicting?) per service, per region, per horizon. Weighted quantile loss for the prediction intervals (is the P90 actually the 90th percentile?). Drift detection tests (is forecast accuracy degrading over time?). I built standardized benchmarking methods for exactly this at Walmart.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake #1: Going Too Deep on ML Too Early

The interview is about system design, not model architecture. Spending 10 minutes explaining LSTM vs. Transformer architectures signals that you're thinking at the wrong level of abstraction. Mention LightGBM/Prophet and move on. The interviewer will ask for depth if they want it вҖ” and when they do, then go deep on your hierarchical reconciliation approach and prediction intervals. Lead with the system, not the model.

Mistake #2: Ignoring the "Distributed" Part

It's tempting to focus only on forecasting and optimization because that's your comfort zone. But the interview is specifically called "Distributed System Design." Force yourself to spend at least 30% of your whiteboard time on infrastructure: how data flows between regions, how services communicate, what happens during network partitions, how you replicate data across AZs. A great forecasting model deployed on a single server is useless for iCloud.

Mistake #3: Presenting a Monolithic System

Don't describe your system as one big application. Show that you understand microservices thinking: the forecaster, the optimizer, the auto-scaler, the monitoring system, and the data pipeline are separate services that communicate via message queues (Kafka) or APIs. Each can be developed, deployed, and scaled independently. This is how real cloud-scale systems work.

Mistake #4: Forgetting the Feedback Loop

A system without monitoring and retraining is incomplete. Always close the loop: forecasts are evaluated against actuals, models are retrained when accuracy degrades, and the optimization parameters are updated based on observed outcomes. If you end your design without discussing monitoring, the interviewer will wonder if you've ever deployed a system in production. You have вҖ” make sure you show it.

Mistake #5: Not Bridging to Your Experience

Other candidates will give textbook answers. You can give battle-tested answers grounded in real systems you've built. Every time you describe a component, add one sentence connecting it to your Walmart or Amazon work. "This is the same approach I used for X at Walmart, where it delivered $Y in savings." Concrete > theoretical.

Mistake #6: Forgetting Cost and Sustainability

The job description explicitly mentions "environmental sustainability and optimal resource utilization." Mentioning power efficiency, renewable energy preferences in region selection, and utilization targets in your MIP constraints will resonate. This isn't just a nice-to-have вҖ” it's a signal that you've read the JD and understand Apple's values.
