Multi-Horizon Capacity Planning Framework

The backbone of any capacity planning system design interview. You must clearly structure decisions by time horizon, showing how short-term tactical actions, medium-term fleet decisions, and long-term capital investments interact through feedback loops.

Short-Term Planning (Minutes → Days)

Short-term capacity planning operates in the realm of immediate demand response. For Apple iCloud, this horizon spans real-time through daily patterns. The core challenge: while cloud infrastructure theoretically offers elasticity, practical constraints in admission control, networking oversubscription, and thermal limits create hard capacity ceilings.

Admission Control & Throttling

Consider iCloud Photo Sync. When a user triggers a photo upload, the system must immediately decide: accept, throttle to a lower-priority tier, or reject. This is governed by an admission control policy monitoring current queue depth \(Q(t)\), available worker threads \(W\), downstream storage replication IOPS, and network ingress bandwidth.

Admission Control Thresholds $$Q(t) > \alpha W \implies \text{Soft throttle (lower-priority queue)}$$ $$Q(t) > \beta W \implies \text{Hard throttle (rate limit ingress)}$$ $$Q(t) > W \implies \text{Reject with exponential backoff}$$ Typical values: α = 0.7, β = 0.9. A Kalman filter forecasts Q(t+Δt) for predictive throttling.

Load Shifting Across Regions

During US peak hours (6–10 PM Pacific), iCloud Photo Sync demand is highest on US-West. Batch ML inference workloads can be migrated to EU-West where utilization is lower. This regional arbitrage is constrained by cross-region network cost (5–10× more per GB than intra-region), latency budgets for batch jobs, and data residency compliance (EU data stays in EU).

Regional Load-Shifting Optimizer $$\max \sum_i c_i(t) \cdot \sum_j B_{ij}(t) - \sum_{i,j} p_{ij} \cdot B_{ij}(t)$$ Where Bij(t) = batch workload shifted from region i to j, pij = per-job migration cost, ci(t) = capacity urgency in region i.

Auto-Scaling Policies

CPU auto-scaling has near-zero deployment latency — VMs can be created and added to load balancers in under a minute. Monitor p95/p99 latency; if either exceeds SLO, spawn new container instances.

GPU auto-scaling is fundamentally different. LLM inference for Apple Intelligence is memory-bound. A 70B-parameter model in FP8 occupies ~140 GB — exceeding a single H100's 80 GB HBM2e. GPU provisioning delay is 5–10 minutes (model weight initialization), and at $15K–$50K per unit, provisioning for a 30-second spike is economically irrational.

Short-term GPU capacity management therefore relies on:

  • Request batching: Queue requests in 100ms windows, process batches of 32 on a single GPU
  • Priority queue management: High-priority Siri queries jump the queue; photo enhancement waits
  • Graceful degradation: If queue depth exceeds threshold, fall back to smaller on-device model

SLO Violation Mitigation

iCloud SLOs are stringent: Photo Sync p99 ≤ 5s, Private Relay p99 ≤ 500ms, iMessage p99 ≤ 2s. When latency spikes above SLO, the system must diagnose within milliseconds:

  • Compute saturation (CPU > 90%) → Shed background jobs (analytics logging)
  • Queue buildup (depth > 1s drain time) → Aggressive throttling
  • Network congestion (packet loss > 0.1%) → Reduce batch sizes, shift traffic across paths
  • GC pauses (> 100ms) → Emergency GC or process restart

Medium-Term Planning (Weeks → Months)

Medium-term planning operates at quarterly to semi-annual horizons, involving fleet composition decisions harder to reverse than throttling but cheaper than capital investment.

Fleet Mix Rebalancing

Apple's infrastructure comprises three primary node types: CPU nodes (~$60–80K, dual-socket AMD EPYC 9004, 192 cores), GPU nodes (~$160–320K, 4–8 H100 GPUs), and Storage nodes (~$50K, 90+ TB per node).

Fleet Mix MILP $$\min \sum_t \left[ c_\text{CPU} \cdot n_{c,t} + c_\text{GPU} \cdot n_{g,t} + c_\text{Stor} \cdot n_{s,t} + M \sum_t y_t \right]$$ Subject to: demand satisfaction per resource type, utilization target ≤ 0.75, lead-time ordering constraints, integer constraints on batch ordering (GPUs ordered in batches of 4/8/16).

Workload Placement Optimization

Apple operates multiple DCs per region (e.g., US-West: Nevada, Northern California, Oregon). Each has different power costs, network topology, user latency profiles, and cooling constraints. The placement problem asks: given services and DCs, how to distribute workloads to minimize latency + network cost + power cost?

Workload Placement LP $$\min \sum_{s,d,d'} \text{traffic}_{s,d,d'} \cdot r_{d,d'} + \sum_{s,d} \text{power-cost}_d \cdot \text{compute}_{s,d}$$ $$\text{s.t.} \quad \sum_d x_{s,d} = 1 \;\forall s, \quad \sum_d x_{s,d} \cdot l_{d,u} \leq L_s \;\forall s,u$$

Edge vs. Centralized Reallocation

Private Relay's two-hop architecture illustrates this tradeoff. Moving Proxy 2 from 5 centralized locations to 50 distributed PoPs reduces network cost from $2.5M/month to $250K/month, but increases operational overhead ($500K/month in staffing) and reliability risk. Net savings: ~$1.6M/month ($19M/year), justifying a 4-quarter rollout plan.

Long-Term Planning (6–24 Months)

Long-term planning is driven by infrastructure capital constraints. A new hyperscale data center takes 18–36 months from site selection to accepting first workloads.

Data Center Build-Out Planning

Site selection criteria: proximity to users (minimize latency), power grid access (50–100 MW continuous), renewable energy availability (Apple's 100% renewable commitment), cooling water/air capacity, redundant fiber routes, regulatory compliance (GDPR, data residency), and geopolitical stability.

Example: Asia-Pacific DC Site Selection

Three candidate sites — Melbourne ($1B, 99% renewable, 10M users), Tokyo ($1.2B, 50% renewable, 40M users, seismic resilience), Singapore ($900M, strategic hub for 200M+ users). Forecast demand 5 years out: \(D_5 = D_0 \cdot (1.20)^5 \cdot (1.15)^5 \approx 5 \times D_0\). If current capacity is 100 PB at 60% utilization, new capacity needed ≈ 533 PB.

Hardware Procurement Lead Times

NVIDIA H100 GPUs: ~16-week lead time (4 months). Custom Apple silicon: 12+ months (design + validation). A typical cycle: forecast demand in Q2, place orders for 20,000 H100s targeting Q4 delivery, deploy in Q1, launch Apple Intelligence with sufficient capacity. If adoption exceeds forecast by >20%, place emergency orders at premium cost.

Renewable Energy & Architecture Shifts

Renewable energy commitments constrain location choices — new DCs must be in regions with available wind/solar capacity. Architecture shifts like ARM-based servers (Apple Silicon for cloud) can reduce power consumption by 40% vs. x86, fundamentally changing the long-term cost model and enabling smaller, more distributed facilities.

The critical design principle: separate forecast input from capacity decision logic. Forecasts produce demand estimates with confidence intervals; the optimizer consumes these as parameters, not as hard constraints. This decoupling prevents the "garbage in, garbage out" problem and allows each component to be improved independently.

Why Feedback Delays Cause Oscillation

If the control loop reacts too aggressively to forecast changes, it creates oscillation: over-provisioning triggers cost alarms → rapid de-provisioning → SLO violations → emergency re-provisioning. Mitigate with deadbands (only scale up if demand exceeds 75% utilization for 2 consecutive weeks) and PID-style control adapted for discrete capacity steps. Target utilization curves should vary by service tier: latency-critical LLM inference targets 60%, bulk storage replication targets 75%.

Planning cycle cadence: Short-term runs continuously (every 5 minutes). Medium-term runs weekly with quarterly deep-dives. Long-term runs quarterly with annual strategic reviews. Each horizon's output constrains the next: long-term budgets bound medium-term hardware availability, which bounds short-term capacity headroom.

Resource Modeling for iCloud Services

Apple cares deeply about physical constraints. Capacity is multidimensional — compute, storage, network — and the bottleneck resource defines effective capacity. You must model resource "shapes" and understand how they couple.

Compute Resources

CPU Cores

A typical iCloud CPU node (dual-socket AMD EPYC 9004, 192 cores) has ~162 allocatable cores after kernel overhead (10–15%), hypervisor (5–10%), and monitoring (2–5 cores). With 10–15% bin-packing fragmentation waste:

CPU Capacity Model $$C_\text{usable} = n \times 162 \times (1 - 0.125) = 142n \text{ cores}$$ $$\text{Utilization target} = 70\% \implies C_\text{available} = 142n \times 0.70 = 99.4n \text{ cores}$$ For a region with 1,000 CPU nodes: 99,400 available cores.

GPU Memory: The Binding Constraint for LLM Inference

An NVIDIA H100 has 80 GB HBM2e at ~3 TB/s bandwidth and 141 TFLOPS FP32. A 70B-parameter LLM in FP8 requires ~70 GB — barely fitting one H100 with ~10 GB for KV-cache and activations. Throughput depends heavily on batch size:

For 10,000 QPS peak demand with batch-32: need 313 H100s. At 70% utilization target: 447 H100s ($13.4M CAPEX).

Custom Apple Silicon

Hypothetical Apple-designed cloud accelerator: 160 GB HBM (hosts 140B model on single chip), 5 TB/s bandwidth, 400W (vs. H100's 700W). Throughput: 64 inferences/s per chip. For 10K QPS: only 156 chips needed. TCO savings over 5 years: $84.5M vs. H100 fleet, justifying $100M R&D investment.

Storage Resources

Capacity Growth Modeling

iCloud Storage Demand Forecast $$D_\text{storage}(t) = N_\text{devices}(t) \times S_\text{avg}(t)$$ $$N_\text{devices}(t) = 1.5\text{B} \times (1.06)^t, \quad S_\text{avg}(t) = 40\text{ GB} \times (1.15)^t$$ $$D_\text{storage}(2029) \approx 161 \text{ PB}$$

Replication & Erasure Coding

Three-way replication stores 3 copies (replication factor 3×). Erasure coding (e.g., 4+2 Reed-Solomon) achieves 1.5× effective replication — saving 2× storage vs. 3-way replication, at the cost of higher decode CPU and latency. Apple likely uses a hybrid approach:

TierAccess PatternReplication StrategyEffective Factor
HotFrequent access (recent photos)3-way replication3.0×
WarmOccasional access(4,2) erasure coding1.5×
ColdRare (account recovery)(6,3) erasure coding1.5×

Blended effective replication factor: ~2.0×. Raw storage needed: 2.0 × 161 PB = 322 PB by 2029.

IOPS and Throughput

SSDs: 50K–100K IOPS, 1–5ms latency. HDDs: 200–500 IOPS, 5–20ms latency. iCloud has strong read/write asymmetry — reads are 10× writes at the regional level. The bottleneck is usually peak write throughput during evening sync hours when millions of users upload photos simultaneously.

Network Resources

Network Tiers

  • Inter-rack: 25–100 Gbps links, 4:1 oversubscription ratio within DC
  • Inter-DC (cross-region): $0.05–$0.10/GB transit, ~150ms transoceanic latency. 10–100 Tbps between major region pairs.
  • Edge-to-user: Last-mile bottleneck (100–500 Mbps home broadband, 20–50 Mbps mobile)

80–90% cache hit rate for photo thumbnails means most viewing traffic never touches the backbone. Apple operates 50–100 CDN PoPs globally, each caching ~10 PB of popular content. This saves enormous backbone bandwidth but adds $250M–$500M in CDN infrastructure investment.

Coupled Constraints & Bottleneck Modeling

Effective capacity is multidimensional. A server's throughput is limited by whichever resource hits its ceiling first:

Bottleneck Capacity Model $$x \leq \min\left(\frac{\text{CPU}}{c_1},\; \frac{\text{GPU}}{c_2},\; \frac{\text{Net}}{c_3},\; \frac{\text{Storage}}{c_4}\right)$$ The bottleneck resource defines effective capacity. All other resources above the bottleneck are "stranded."
Stranded Capacity Example

A rack has 60% CPU utilization but 95% memory utilization. The remaining 40% CPU is stranded — unusable because memory is exhausted. Similarly, a DC with 1,000 GPUs but only 900 NVMe drives for model caching must leave 100 GPUs idle. Stranded capacity represents pure waste in your cost model.

Solutions: (1) joint procurement (balanced GPU + memory + NVMe packages), (2) rebalancing via racking changes, (3) workload shifting (route non-memory-intensive work to memory-constrained nodes). In your MIP, model paired resources and penalize imbalance: add constraint \(y_r \geq 0.9 \times x_r\) ensuring each GPU has sufficient NVMe backing.

Cost Modeling (Critical for Apple)

The job description explicitly mentions cost modeling. You must think in $/unit of service and demonstrate fluency with CAPEX, OPEX, depreciation, energy costs, and shadow prices from LP duals.

Cost Types: CAPEX & OPEX

CAPEX (Capital Expenditure)
  • DC Construction: $600M–$1.2B per hyperscale facility
  • Server/GPU purchase: CPU node $60–80K, GPU node $160–320K, Storage node $50–70K
  • Networking equipment: $200M–$500M per DC
  • Custom silicon R&D: $100M–$300M per generation
OPEX (Operating Expenditure)
  • Power & cooling: ~$19M/year per 60 MW DC (PUE 1.15–1.20)
  • Labor: ~$20M/year per DC (100 FTE)
  • Hardware replacement: ~$105M/year (2–5% failure rate)
  • Network transit: $12M–$120M/year

Horizon interaction: Short-term is almost pure OPEX (spin up VMs, pay for power). Medium-term is hybrid (purchase servers to reduce per-unit costs). Long-term is dominated by CAPEX (pour concrete, buy GPUs with 6–12 month lead times).

Cost per Inference Request

For an H100 serving 32 inferences/second (batch-32, 70B model): GPU depreciation ($0.000001/req) + power ($0.0000013/req) + interconnect ($0.000001/req) + network/storage ($0.000001/req) = ~$0.000005/inference, or $0.50 per million inferences. At 10K QPS, infrastructure cost is ~$1.6M/year.

Cost per Active Device

The metric Apple leadership tracks. Total iCloud infrastructure cost / 1.5B active devices ≈ $8–12 per device per year. This informs pricing strategy and R&D investment decisions.

Depreciation Modeling

Straight-Line Depreciation $$\text{Annual Depreciation} = \frac{\text{Hardware Cost}}{\text{Useful Life (years)}}$$ CPU node ($70K, 5-year life) → $14K/year. GPU ($30K, 3-year life due to obsolescence) → $10K/year.

Vintage Effects

Older hardware is fully depreciated (book value $0) so its incremental cost is just power + maintenance (~$5K/year vs. $14K/year for new). This incentivizes keeping old hardware — but it's less efficient (20% more power, 10% less throughput). The true TCO must account for depreciation, power, maintenance, performance degradation, and opportunity cost of datacenter real estate.

Double-Declining Balance (DDB)

Front-loads depreciation: Year 1 = $28K, Year 2 = $16.8K, Year 3 = $10K, Year 4 = $6K, Year 5 = $3.6K. Better reflects GPU value curves where technology leapfrogs make older GPUs less valuable quickly.

Energy Cost Modeling

Power Cost Model $$\text{Annual Power Cost} = P_\text{peak} \times \text{CF} \times 8760 \times \text{Price}_\text{kWh}$$ For a 60 MW DC at 60% capacity factor, $0.06/kWh: $18.9M/year. PUE of 1.20 adds 20% overhead for cooling.

GPU-heavy LLM inference is power-intensive: 447 H100s × 700W = 313 kW IT power. With PUE 1.20: 375 kW facility power. Annual energy cost: ~$138M for a 10K QPS inference service.

Renewable PPAs

Apple's 100% renewable commitment uses long-term Power Purchase Agreements: 10–20 year contracts at $0.04–$0.07/kWh (cheaper than escalating grid rates). Over 20 years, renewable PPAs save ~40% vs. retail grid electricity.

Carbon-Aware Scheduling

Grid carbon intensity varies hourly (500–800 g CO₂/kWh during fossil peak, 50–200 during renewable peak). Shift deferrable batch workloads to low-carbon windows. This creates a multi-objective optimization: minimize both cost and carbon emissions, subject to latency SLOs.

Shadow Prices & Marginal Cost

Shadow Prices from LP Dual Variables

If the dual variable for the GPU constraint is high (e.g., λGPU = $15/GPU/year) but storage is low (λstor = $2/TB/year), GPUs are the economic bottleneck. Each additional GPU saves $15 in system cost — directly informing procurement priorities. Use duals to:

  • Prioritize hardware procurement (invest where duals are highest)
  • Negotiate budget (argue for CAPEX if dual on budget constraint μ > 0.2, meaning $1 CAPEX → $5 OPEX savings)
  • Guide build-vs-buy decisions (if dual on own-DC constraint > colocation cost, build more DCs)

Marginal vs. fixed cost: Short-term scaling cost is mostly marginal (each additional VM costs power + licensing). Long-term expansion has large fixed costs ($600M DC) amortized across many units. The optimization must use amortized cost, not just marginal — otherwise it under-invests in infrastructure that has high upfront cost but low per-unit marginal cost.

Optimization Formulations

This is your strength as an optimization practitioner. Lean into LP, MIP, bin packing, robust optimization, and decomposition methods — all applied to iCloud capacity planning.

Linear Programming for Workload Allocation

The canonical workload allocation is a multi-commodity flow problem. Let \(w \in W\) be workloads (Photo Sync, LLM Inference, Private Relay), \(i \in R\) be regions, \(d_w\) demand, \(C_{i,w}\) capacity, \(c_{i,w}\) cost, and \(x_{i,w}\) the fraction of workload \(w\) served from region \(i\).

Workload Allocation LP $$\min \sum_{w \in W} \sum_{i \in R} d_w \cdot x_{i,w} \cdot c_{i,w}$$ $$\text{s.t.} \quad \sum_{w} d_w \cdot x_{i,w} \leq C_{i,w} \;\;\forall i \quad \text{(capacity)}$$ $$\sum_{i} L_{u,i} \cdot x_{i,w} \leq L_{\max,w} \;\;\forall u,w \quad \text{(latency SLO)}$$ $$\sum_{i} x_{i,w} = 1 \;\;\forall w, \quad x_{i,w} \geq 0$$

Dual Variable Interpretation

The dual \(\lambda_i\) on capacity constraints = marginal value of one more unit of capacity in region \(i\). The dual \(\mu_w\) on demand = marginal cost of serving one more unit of workload \(w\). If \(\lambda_\text{US-West}\) is 3× \(\lambda_\text{EU}\), prioritize GPU investment in US-West.

Concrete Example: 5-Region Allocation

Allocating Photo Sync (500 PB/mo), LLM Inference (50M req/day), and Private Relay (2 Pbps) across US-West, US-East, EU, Asia-Pacific, Canada. LP reveals: US-West is GPU-constrained (high λ), Canada has cheapest peering for Relay, EU has GDPR compliance surcharge. Dual analysis directly informs where to invest next.

Mixed Integer Programming (MIP)

Binary decisions require integer variables: build a DC (\(y_k \in \{0,1\}\)), activate GPU cluster, select hardware type. MIP captures the "lumpiness" of infrastructure — you can't build half a data center.

Fixed-Charge Facility Location MIP $$\min \sum_{i} F_i \cdot y_i + \sum_{w,i} d_w \cdot x_{i,w} \cdot c_{i,w}$$ $$\text{s.t.} \quad \sum_{w} d_w \cdot x_{i,w} \leq M \cdot y_i \;\;\forall i \quad \text{(big-M linking)}$$ $$x_{i,w} \leq y_i \;\;\forall i,w \quad \text{(can only serve if built)}$$ $$y_i \in \{0,1\}, \quad x_{i,w} \geq 0$$ Fi = annualized fixed cost of DC in region i ($50–200M/year). M = upper bound on total demand (big-M).

Hardware Type Selection

Choose between GPU types: \(h_{i,t} \in \mathbb{Z}_+\) = number of hardware type \(t\) (H100, A100, custom Apple silicon) in region \(i\). Each type has different cost, memory, throughput, and power profiles. The MIP selects the cost-minimizing fleet mix.

MIP Gap and Solver Strategy

Branch-and-bound with cutting planes (CPLEX, Gurobi). For strategic planning, a 1–2% MIP gap is often acceptable — the model uncertainty exceeds solver imprecision. Typical solve times: 100-variable MIP in seconds, 10,000-variable in minutes to hours.

Bin Packing for Server Allocation

Multi-dimensional bin packing is NP-hard: pack workloads (each requiring CPU + memory + GPU + network) into heterogeneous servers. Each workload is a vector \(\mathbf{r}_j = (r_j^\text{CPU}, r_j^\text{mem}, r_j^\text{GPU}, r_j^\text{net})\) and each server is a bin with capacity vector \(\mathbf{B}_i\).

Heuristics

Fragmentation Metrics

Fragmentation ratio = (total free capacity − max contiguous free block) / total free capacity. If fragmentation > 20%, trigger workload compaction during low-traffic windows. In your MIP, model fragmentation as a waste factor: \(C_\text{eff} = (1 - w) \times C_\text{nominal}\) where \(w \approx 0.10\text{–}0.20\).

Robust Optimization

Instead of point forecasts, use uncertainty sets:

Robust Capacity Planning $$\min_x \max_{D \in \mathcal{D}} \;\text{cost}(x, D)$$ $$\mathcal{D} = \left\{ D : D_r = \hat{D}_r(1+\delta_r),\; |\delta_r| \leq 0.15,\; \sum_r |\delta_r| \leq \Gamma \right\}$$ Budget of uncertainty Γ ≈ 1.2–1.5 prevents all regions spiking simultaneously. Choose Γ so actual demands stay within D 99% of the time.

Decomposition for Scalability

A monolithic global MIP won't scale — Apple has dozens of regions, hundreds of service types, thousands of server types. Decomposition is essential.

Three Decomposition Axes

1. Temporal Decomposition (Rolling Horizon)

Solve next 3 months at weekly granularity, months 3–12 at monthly, years 1–3 at quarterly. Link via boundary conditions: the 3-month solution respects capacity constraints from the long-term roadmap.

2. Spatial Decomposition (Dual/Lagrangian)

Partition into regional clusters (US, EMEA, APAC). Solve each region's MIP independently. Relax coupling constraints (cross-region latency, replication) into the objective via Lagrangian multipliers. Iterate via subgradient optimization until multipliers converge.

3. Service-Level Decomposition

Partition by workload type (LLM inference, storage, backup). Master problem allocates shared resources; service subproblems optimize within their allocation. Uses Benders decomposition to separate integer (strategic) from continuous (operational) decisions.

Combined: temporal (3 levels) × spatial (5–7 clusters) × service (5 categories) = ~90 tractable subproblems, each solvable in seconds. Re-solve the monolithic problem quarterly to measure and correct decomposition drift.

Service-Specific Planning Nuances

"Forecast accuracy is necessary but not sufficient. What matters is mapping demand into resource-constrained, latency-aware allocation." Show the interviewer you understand how different iCloud workloads demand fundamentally different planning approaches.

iCloud Storage

iCloud Storage has a steady, predictable growth curve driven by device sales and storage tier adoption. It's the easiest service to forecast but hardest to provision quickly (storage hardware has long lead times).

Key Planning Characteristics

  • Replication factor: 3-way for hot data, erasure coding for cold — hybrid approach optimizes cost vs. latency
  • Hot/warm/cold tiering: Photos accessed frequently in first week (hot → SSD), then rapidly cool. Model data temperature distribution to size each tier.
  • Migration planning: Moving data between tiers/regions without disrupting service. Background migration during off-peak windows at controlled I/O rates.
  • Metadata servers: Billions of file records require their own capacity plan — metadata grows faster than data if file count increases faster than file size.
  • Deduplication/compression: 20–30% savings on raw capacity. Must model compression ratios that vary by content type (photos compress 10%, already-compressed video: 2%).

Private Relay

Network bandwidth is the primary constraint, not compute. The two-hop architecture (user → Proxy 1 → Proxy 2 → destination) means capacity planning must account for both hops independently.

Unique Challenges

  • Encryption CPU overhead: TLS handshakes and data encryption consume significant CPU — each connection requires ~5ms of crypto compute, limiting per-core connection rates.
  • Geo-distributed load: Users expect relay nodes within 50ms, requiring 50+ PoPs globally. Small PoPs have less statistical multiplexing → higher variance in utilization.
  • Peering relationships: Egress cost optimization is critical. A well-negotiated peering agreement can reduce transit costs by 80%.
  • Traffic patterns: Correlated with browsing activity (evening peaks, geographic patterns). Weekend traffic is 20–30% lower than weekdays.

Private Compute Cloud

Private Compute Cloud (PCC) runs sensitive ML inference in Apple's secure enclaves, with strict isolation guarantees.

Planning Challenges

  • Burst traffic: Triggered by on-device events (Siri queries, photo analysis, writing suggestions). Pareto-distributed — 10% of users generate 70% of requests.

Q1. How would you design a multi-horizon capacity planning system for Apple Intelligence's LLM inference infrastructure?

Follow-up: What triggers a full replanning cycle vs. incremental updates?

Topic 4: Optimization Formulations
Q7. Formulate the data center expansion problem as a MIP. What are the key decision variables, constraints, and objective?

Define the problem over a 12-month horizon with monthly time steps \(t\) and regions \(r\). Decision variables: \(x_{r,t}\) = GPU units procured (continuous), \(z_{r,t} \in \{0,1\}\) = build new DC facility, \(s_{r,t}\) = GPU inventory at month end. Objective: \(\min \sum_{r,t} [c_r x_{r,t} + f_r z_{r,t} + h_r s_{r,t} + p_r u_{r,t}]\) minimizing procurement, facility, holding, and operational costs. Key constraints: (1) inventory balance: \(s_{r,t} = s_{r,t-1} + x_{r,t} - u_{r,t}\); (2) demand satisfaction: \(u_{r,t} \geq D_{r,t}\); (3) facility linking: \(u_{r,t} \leq C_r \cdot z_{r,t}\); (4) lead time: orders at \(t\) available at \(t + \tau\); (5) global budget: \(\sum c_r x_{r,t} \leq B\). This is a convex MIP solvable in seconds for 4 regions × 12 months via branch-and-bound. For 20+ regions, I'd use Lagrangian relaxation or Benders decomposition to maintain tractability.

Follow-up: How do you incorporate demand uncertainty?
Extend to stochastic MIP: add scenario dimension ω ∈ {base, high, low} with probabilities. Replace deterministic D with D(ω) and solve for expected cost across scenarios. This yields more robust facility decisions that perform well across demand outcomes.
Follow-up: Can you reuse hardware from decommissioned DCs?
Yes — add variable y(r,t) = refurbished GPUs from retiring facilities. Modify inventory balance to include y(r,t) as supply. Penalize with a slight cost premium (lower reliability, older technology) but allow if the dual value justifies it.
Q8. A monolithic optimization for all of Apple's capacity planning won't scale. How would you decompose the problem?

I'd combine three decomposition axes. Temporal: solve next 3 months at weekly granularity, months 3–12 at monthly, years 1–3 at quarterly — linked via boundary conditions where each horizon's output constrains the next. Spatial: partition into 5–7 regional clusters (US, EMEA, APAC, etc.), solve each region's MIP independently, then use Lagrangian relaxation to handle cross-region coupling constraints (replication, latency). Iterate via subgradient optimization until multipliers converge. Service-level: partition by workload type (LLM inference, storage, relay), solve a master problem that allocates shared resources, then service subproblems optimize within their allocation. Combined: 3 temporal levels × 6 spatial clusters × 5 service categories = ~90 tractable subproblems, each solvable in seconds. I validate optimality by solving the full monolithic problem quarterly (8–12 hour runtime) and measuring the gap — if decomposition gap exceeds 5%, tighten coupling cuts.

Follow-up: How do you ensure the decomposed solution stays close to globally optimal?
Solve the LP relaxation of the full problem as a lower bound and compare against the decomposed solution. If the gap is <5%, decomposition is acceptable. Add coupling cuts to tighten if needed.
Follow-up: How often do you re-couple subproblems to prevent drift?
Weekly/monthly: solve decomposed version. Quarterly: re-solve full monolithic problem to detect and correct suboptimality. This amortizes computational cost while bounding cumulative drift to <3%.
Q9. How would you apply robust optimization to handle demand uncertainty in GPU capacity planning?

I'd formulate as \(\min_x \max_{D \in \mathcal{D}} \text{cost}(x, D)\) where the uncertainty set uses a budget-of-uncertainty approach: \(\mathcal{D} = \{D : D_r = \hat{D}_r(1+\delta_r), |\delta_r| \leq 0.15, \sum_r |\delta_r| \leq \Gamma\}\). The budget parameter \(\Gamma \approx 1.2\text{–}1.5\) prevents the unrealistic scenario of all regions spiking simultaneously by 15%. I calibrate \(\Gamma\) from historical data so actual demands stay within \(\mathcal{D}\) 99% of the time. The inner maximization is a linear program solvable analytically via duality, yielding a tractable outer minimization. I implement as iterative cutting planes: solve the MIP for current \(x\), check if capacity holds for worst-case demand (solve the inner LP), if violated add a cut and re-solve. Robust optimization typically provisions 15–20% more than deterministic. To control conservatism, I use a "controllable robust" variant where mid-horizon adjustments are allowed as demand reveals itself — reducing the total over-provision to ~10%.

Follow-up: How do you choose the budget of uncertainty Γ?
Compute realized forecast errors over the past 12 months; fit a distribution and choose Γ such that actual demands remain in the uncertainty set 99% of the time. For iCloud's relatively stable services, Γ ≈ 1.2. For volatile LLM inference, Γ ≈ 1.8.
Follow-up: Doesn't robust optimization over-provision too much?
It can — typically 15–20% more than stochastic optimization. Mitigate by lowering Γ or using adaptive robust where you adjust capacity mid-horizon as demand reveals itself. Compare robust vs. stochastic solutions and choose based on the cost asymmetry of under- vs. over-provisioning.

Q14. How does GPU memory fragmentation affect effective capacity, and what strategies would you recommend?

GPU memory fragmentation occurs when the largest contiguous free block can't accommodate a new model, even though aggregate free memory is sufficient. For example: an H100 has 80 GB; after allocating 15 × 5 GB models scattered across memory, the largest free block is only 5 GB — insufficient for a 10 GB model despite 5 GB total free. For Apple's LLM inference (70B model = 140 GB across 4 H100s in tensor parallel), fragmentation is severe and causes 10–25% effective capacity loss. My strategies: (1) Compaction — periodically defragment by re-allocating models contiguously (100–500ms latency spike, schedule at 2–4 AM); (2) Memory pooling — allocate in fixed-size buckets (10/20/40 GB), trading flexibility for zero fragmentation; (3) Fragmentation-aware scheduling — use bin-packing FFD that considers not just "does it fit?" but "does placing it here create future fragmentation?"; (4) Paged attention (vLLM-style) — non-contiguous KV-cache allocation eliminates sequence-level fragmentation. In the MIP, model this as \(C_\text{eff} = (1-w) \times C_\text{nominal}\) with \(w = 0.20\). Monitor fragmentation ratio weekly; if persistently >15%, trigger compaction and adjust \(w\) upward in procurement planning.

Follow-up: How do you measure fragmentation in production?
Instrument GPU memory allocators to log per-GPU: total allocated, total free, and max-contiguous-free-block every hour. Fragmentation ratio = (total_free − max_free_block) / total_free. Alert if ratio exceeds 20% for any cluster.
Follow-up: Can you intentionally tolerate fragmentation to avoid compaction overhead?
No — the economics don't work. Tolerating 20% fragmentation on 100K GPUs wastes ~$2M/year of capacity, while compaction overhead is <$100K/year in operational cost. Always defragment.
Q15. Design a disaster recovery plan that handles simultaneous regional failures and supply chain shocks.

I'd structure DR in three tiers. Tier 1 (single-region failure, ~1×/year): 25% global headroom absorbs traffic redistribution, cloud burst contracts provide 100K GPUs within 24 hours, automated geo-DNS failover tested quarterly. Tier 2 (multi-region simultaneous, ~1×/decade): air-gapped backups in geographically isolated cold storage (1-week recovery), "survival mode" where APIs return cached responses and writes queue locally until regions recover, service prioritization shedding analytics first, core Mail/Photos last. Tier 3 (supply chain shock, GPU shortage >6 months): multi-vendor strategy (30% AMD/AWS Trainium), long-term supply contracts with NVIDIA locking 60% at fixed prices, hardware-agnostic inference stacks (portable across NVIDIA/AMD/custom), and graceful degradation — shift to CPU-based inference (1000× slower, acceptable for batch) and dynamic pricing to shed low-value requests. Triggers: region down >6 hours → Tier 1; 2+ regions down → Tier 2; GPU lead times >12 months → Tier 3. Annual DR drills simulate Tier 2 (3 regions offline) measuring recovery time, cost, and user-facing impact.

Follow-up: How do you choose which services to shed first?
Compute an essentiality score = criticality × revenue_impact × SLA_penalty. Rank: Mail/Photos (100), Cloud Backup (80), LLM Inference (50), Analytics (10). Shed lowest-score first. Update scores quarterly as product mix evolves.
Follow-up: Is 7-day recovery acceptable for cold storage?
For compliance archives, yes. For user data recovery (device loss), no — implement tiered recovery: hot backup (1-hour RTO), warm (1-day), cold (7-day). Most users hit the hot or warm tier; cold is for rare disaster recovery scenarios only.
